0. Status and limits

This is not a final class-approved design. It is a disciplined design basis: dimensions, architecture, assumptions, system boundaries, risks, and the engineering roadmap required before FEED. Numerical values are preliminary and must be validated by hydrostatics, structural finite-element analysis, seakeeping analysis, evacuation simulation, model tests, class review, and regulatory negotiation.

Source hierarchy used: current official Freedom Ship public dimensions are treated as controlling where they conflict with older third-party descriptions. Older public values are retained only as context.

2. Design intent and governing philosophy

Design type: modular, cellular, slow-speed, ocean-going floating city platform with distributed propulsion, redundant municipal-scale utilities, segregated urban districts, and continuous maintainability.

Primary objectives

  • Permanent habitability: motion, vibration, acoustic comfort, daylight, air quality, and daily logistics dominate over cruise-style novelty.
  • Survivability: local flooding, fire, machinery loss, cyberattack, medical emergency, or severe weather must not escalate into whole-city loss.
  • Maintainability: normal drydocking is not credible at this scale; design must support underwater inspection, cofferdams, replaceable modules, and robotic hull maintenance.
  • Mobility: slow global movement with weather routing, seasonal route planning, and safe loitering.
  • Regulatory acceptability: class/flag/IMO-equivalent safety basis must be negotiated from day one.

3. Hull, structure, hydrostatics, and seakeeping basis

Hull architecture

Use a modular cellular displacement hull, not a conventional slender passenger-ship hull. The basic architecture should include:

  • deep double-bottom cellular pontoon;
  • longitudinal buoyancy lanes and transverse watertight bulkheads;
  • side ballast and damage-stability compartments;
  • protected sea chests and redundant seawater intakes;
  • sacrificial outer shell/fender/impact zones;
  • service and utility tunnels above machinery level.

Structural verification basis

  • Still-water and wave-induced global bending moments.
  • Oblique-sea torsion and long-span global flexure.
  • Module-joint fatigue and residual-stress management.
  • Slamming, green-water, bow/quarter impact, and accidental collision/grounding.
  • Progressive-collapse resistance after local fire/flood/impact damage.
Stability principle: maximize safety, not GM. Very high GM from the wide beam can drive uncomfortable roll accelerations. The target is safe but comfortable GM using low CG, ballast control, passive/active damping, and controlled mass distribution.

Required hydrodynamic program

  1. Hydrostatic model and weight estimate.
  2. Potential-flow diffraction/radiation model.
  3. Global wave-load and spectral fatigue model.
  4. CFD for bow, stern, internal harbor, wake, and maneuvering effects.
  5. Physical model basin testing.
  6. Maneuvering and station-keeping simulation.
  7. Route-weather operability simulation.
  8. Motion-comfort assessment against habitable-space acceleration criteria.

4. System architecture

Propulsion and maneuvering

  • Distributed electric propulsion with multiple separated machinery rooms.
  • Azimuthing pods at stern/quarters plus selected bow/side thrusters for low-speed control.
  • Dynamic-positioning-like control for harbor interface and station keeping; not full offshore DP dependency for normal city life.
  • Design service speed 6–8 kn; maximum 10 kn only if resistance/power/structural studies justify it.

Power and microgrid

Renewables are useful supplements, not credible baseload for a city-scale permanent platform. The dependable basis should be firm distributed generation plus storage and renewables.

  • Dual-fuel engines/turbines: LNG, methanol, or renewable-diesel capable.
  • Battery energy storage for load smoothing and critical ride-through.
  • Flywheels for transient stabilization.
  • Solar/wind/wave-energy features as supplemental sources after maintainability and safety screening.
  • Waste-heat recovery for desalination and district thermal systems.
  • Islandable grid zones: propulsion, residential/hotel, hospital/life safety, data/comms, industrial/services, emergency.

Water, wastewater, and waste

  • Reverse-osmosis desalination plus thermal desalination from waste heat.
  • Potable, non-potable, firefighting, chilled-water, and hot-water loops with district isolation.
  • Greywater reuse, blackwater treatment, MBR/tertiary/UV-ozone polishing.
  • Source-separated solid waste: food, recyclables, hazardous, medical, e-waste, sludge, oily waste.
  • Residual waste must be containerized and compliantly offloaded; “zero waste” is an aspiration, not a design guarantee.

Food and agriculture

Hydroponics/aeroponics/bio-harvest systems should support fresh produce, education, and partial resilience. They should not be assumed to fully feed 60,000–100,000 people without a dedicated agricultural area/power/water model.

Mobility hierarchy

  1. Pedestrian-first central boulevard/atrium and neighborhood streets.
  2. Automated longitudinal people mover with stops at major zones.
  3. Segregated service logistics deck using AGVs/electric carts.
  4. Separate waste/goods/emergency routes from resident and visitor circulation.
  5. Stretcher-capable vertical circulation and protected emergency stairs.

6. Life safety, health, security, and external access

Fire and evacuation philosophy

Lifeboats alone are not a credible city-scale safety concept. The primary safety case must prevent incident propagation, shelter people in protected districts, move occupants internally first, and use external evacuation only when required.
  • District-level fire/flood/smoke compartments.
  • Protected refuge areas and refuge floors by zone.
  • Redundant evacuation corridors and vertical circulation.
  • Onboard professional fire brigade, medical response, and public-safety command.
  • Sprinkler/water-mist systems, smoke extraction, emergency lighting, multilingual alerting.
  • Crowd-flow simulation and regular district drills.

Healthcare basis

A real floating city needs a municipal health system: primary clinics by district, emergency department, surgical capability, ICU, maternity, dental, pharmacy, diagnostics, imaging, mental health, infectious disease isolation, morgue, and medevac interface.

Security basis

  • Resident credentialing and visitor access segmentation.
  • Arrival screening, customs/immigration integration, contraband control.
  • Cyber-physical security operations center.
  • CCTV with privacy governance, tender tracking, drone detection, maritime exclusion monitoring.
  • High-risk cargo and medical quarantine isolation.

External access

Assume the platform rarely docks conventionally. Normal access is offshore anchorage/station plus protected internal harbor, tenders, ferries, cargo barges, service boats, and helicopter medevac. The artificial harbor is a mission-critical system and must be analyzed for waves, wakes, traffic, disabled-craft recovery, security, and emergency operations.

7. Class, regulatory, and environmental basis

The platform is a hybrid of ship, offshore installation, floating city, special-purpose passenger vessel, and municipal infrastructure. Existing rule sets will not fully cover it.

Required engagement

  • Flag state and class society: ABS, DNV, Lloyd's Register, Bureau Veritas, or equivalent.
  • IMO/SOLAS equivalency review for life safety, subdivision, fire, evacuation, and safe-return/safe-loitering.
  • MARPOL compliance for waste, sewage, ballast, oily water, and emissions.
  • Port-state control, customs/immigration, health/quarantine, aviation regulators, insurers, and labor authorities.

Environmental basis

  • District HVAC with seawater heat exchange, humidity control, smoke isolation, and hospital-grade zones.
  • Corrosion control: coatings, impressed-current cathodic protection, monitoring, splash-zone hardening, and robotic inspection.
  • Noise/vibration control: machinery raft mounting, acoustic breaks, resilient residential floors, quiet propulsors, and service-corridor isolation.

9. Phased engineering roadmap

  1. Feasibility and rule-basis phase: confirm dimensions, mission profile, route, class/flag basis, population cases, financing, and environmental strategy.
  2. Concept validation phase: hydrostatics, weight budget, preliminary structure, seakeeping, power/water/waste sizing, life-safety concept.
  3. Prototype module: build and test a representative hull/superstructure/utility/residential module.
  4. Pilot platform: join 2–4 modules and prove harbor, microgrid, desalination, wastewater, transit, and emergency systems.
  5. Core spine build: arrival zone, city core, control systems, central utilities, and marine operations.
  6. Residential expansion: add neighborhoods in controlled blocks with commissioning gates.
  7. Hospitality/event expansion: add hotels, convention, waterpark, and stadium only after core systems prove reliable.
  8. Full operational maturity: continuous upgrade, inspection, module replacement, and route/port operations.

10. Immediate next engineering work packages

  1. Weight and area model: deck-by-deck area, mass, CG, payload, reserves, and growth margin.
  2. Hydrostatic baseline: displacement, draft alternatives, GM targets, ballast envelope, damage cases.
  3. Structural concept model: primary girder/cell layout, module joints, global bending/torsion envelopes.
  4. Seakeeping feasibility model: preliminary RAOs, accelerations, route-weather operability, damping strategy.
  5. Life-safety concept: district compartments, refuge capacities, evacuation fleet assumptions, fire/smoke zones.
  6. Utilities sizing: firm power, fuel, water, wastewater, solid waste, HVAC, emergency reserves.
  7. Regulatory basis memorandum: proposed classification path and SOLAS/MARPOL equivalency matrix.
